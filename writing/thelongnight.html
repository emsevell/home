The Long Night (Draft 1)

by emsevell

In the years when the first of the great uranium-steam generators were built, when the electron-tube was still new and the thinking-towers were still just a dream of iron and glass, the world was yet untamed. Even as the great American scientists and scholars of the East unravelled the mysteries of the age and harnessed new energies they did not understand and could barely control, some were not satisfied. Many, be it for greed, desperation, or for the sake of adventure itself, turned their eyes to the West, to the lands beyond the prairies, the lands of the dark woods and mountains.

The West was as wild as it was unknown. Of the explorers bold enough to enter the great frontier, not many emerged again. Some returned in the throes of madness, no longer able to tell friend and enemy apart. Some returned as hollow, shambling things with empty eyes, dead even as they walked and ate and spoke. But the few who still had their wits about them told stories of terror and woe - the ones who didn't refuse to speak of their journey at all, at least.

They told of restless ghosts that wandered the trees, eager to snatch a man's soul. They told of the hell-dogs and the wendigo of the mountains, and the strange arts of the valley shaman who would as soon cut your throat as offer you a place at their fire.

Many believed them, and stayed far away from the land across the great river. But a man named Donnan did not. "Ghost stories", he would say gruffly, "are for children, to frighten them in their beds." He wasn't a particularly large man, but his face was hard and his eyes made you know that he would fight you if you crossed him wrong. His chest was deep and his arms were strong, and he had a great big brown beard with a hint of red in it. He wouldn't call himself a proud man, but he was proud of his beard.

He had been staying in a small town, just twenty miles east of the river. He was a hunter, and work had been very slow. There was little game to be found there, only just enough to survive himself, and it was just becoming deep winter. His pockets were empty, and his chances were quickly running out. So when he heard of a caravan preparing to embark to the west, offering good payment for 'diverse skills and strengths', he went at once to see if it was a hunter they were looking for.

He was met by a man who Donnan assumed to be the leader. He was an Irishman much like himself, perhaps first or second generation, and Donnan found him agreeable at once. He had a kind face, clean-shaven, and a smile that beamed at him out from under a misshapen dumpling of a hat. It was the sort of face that made you feel like the most important person in the room.

"'Ello there!" The sturdy man reached out a bear-like hand to grip Donnan's in a firm handshake. Donnan noted that his hands, though large, were relatively un-calloused. "I'll be Hammond O'Neil. You'll be looking for the job, then?"

Donnan met the man's, Hammond's, joviality with a smile of his own. Or at least he gave it his best try. "Aye. You'll be the head of this caravan, then?"

Hammond grinned at him, pleased. "Aye and again!" he roared happily. "The O'Neil party, they've taken to calling us. No finer a fifty you'll find anywhere, and cattle-head to match."

Donnan nodded in appreciation, trying to look impressed at a caravan he did not care two cents about. He wasn't particularly interested in a long journey out to the West, but he didn't have many choices left. "Good, then. Is it a hunter you need?" He gestured to the long rifle slung over his shoulder.

Hammond squinted at him with interest. "Right to business, then. That's just like you hunters, yanno. Never easy to distract. I always liked that." He rubbed his stubbly chin with one hand. "I think a hunter's just what we're after, here. We're putting together an advance party, see. I've word the pass is blocked by this month's storm, and it's the work of four or five good men to clear."

Surprised, Donnan looked at him anew. This, at least, was interesting.

Hammond continued. "It's near enough to reach in a hard march's day, even in the weather. You'd be get out there, clear the pass, then head right the way back. You and a few others, that is." He tapped against his chin absently. His smile had faded as he spoke, if only to make way for a look of fierce concentration that deeply furrowed his brow. "There's a cabin near enough, built for just a time as this. You'll find it stocked well."

Donnan chewed on that for a moment. He'd heard of the pass before, a passage through the rocky lands just to the west of the river. "A few others?" he asked slowly.

"Ah, indeed!" Hammond's smile leapt back to his face at once. "Four others like yourselves, ready to strike out for the near west! Hearty fellows, you'll find them." He looked Donnan over, letting his eyes, which were rather small, fall on the small bag that he kept at his side. "Need ye make any preparations? Close up shop? Give yer sweet a kiss?"

"No." He shook his head. "Already closed up here. This is all I've need for." He wasn’t about to admit that it was all he had to his name now.

"Excellent!" Hammond clapped his hands excitedly and jumped out of his chair. "I'll bring ya to meet them at once! Then lodging for the night, and off you'll get!"

"And payment?" Donnan asked shortly.

"Payment! Of course." Hammond sank back down into his chair chortling. "Never distracted, you hunters. Let's talk it over, then?"

-------

The rest of that day and night passed quickly. Donnan was quickly bustled to the nearby inn, where he was introduced to the others.

The first he met was the Farmer. He was a burly man, obviously a worker of the fields even under his coat and low hat. He was well-used to the type of work they would be doing, Hammond told him, and was going to be the leader of this little expedition.

"The leader?" Donnan had asked, surprised.

"Oh, yes," Hammond replied. "He's a long hand in keeping his flocks and helpers together on the farm - you'll find him more than able to take up the front on this one."

Donnan's surprise quickly turned to apallment. He couldn't really have said he expected there to be a leader chosen at all, but to find that there was one - and one such as a simple Farmer, no less! - galled him to the core. His temperament had quite been soured, even though it would only be for a short few days. Nevertheless, he swallowed his anger and stepped up to look the Farmer up and down. His milky-grey eyes peered out at him from under a misshapen wool hat.

"Hard times?" he asked quietly.

The Farmer nodded. "Bad crop," he stated simply. "Nothing grows well, this close to the West. Nothing from the East likes it here. Doesn't belong."

Donnan frowned at the Farmer's superstition, but held his tongue. It would have to do.

The rest were quickly introduced as well. There was a wiry Surveyor, a young Sailor, and, most unusual of all, a Mountain-Man. What a Sailor was doing this far west, Donnan could only guess. Perhaps shipping on the river. But here he was.

Once they had all been introduced with firm handshakes and stiff nods, Hammond ladened them all with supplies for the journey. "Courtesy of the party, of course, of course," he was saying. "Now get your rest. If you set off before dawn, you'll arrive well before the sunset." For it was now night.

Donnan retreated to his room. An uneasy feeling had sunk into the pit of his stomach, only worsening as he prepared. Many times on the hunt, he had heeded such a feeling, an instinct honed over decades of life on the edge of danger. It had kept him from pitfalls and predators in the past. And now the same feeling sat heavy in his gut, and he didn't like it one bit.

--------

Donnan rose the next morning before the sun. It was a crisp and clear winter's morning. Everything seemed still - there was no wind at all, and the sky was as starless and motionless as the ink-black waters of the river below. The whole village seemed to hold its breath. Even the few torches and lanterns scattered about refused to flicker. It was the sort of morning where nothing changed, one that would keep you awake if you were awake, and keep you asleep if you were asleep. And yet, here he was, bundling on his warmest clothes and stumbling out the door on stiff and sleepy legs.

The five of them ate a cold breakfast and then disembarked without a word. Hammond had not shown up again that morning, but from the way the others were looking at the Surveyor Donnan assumed that the small man had been given directions. Sure enough, he smartly led the way, stamping along the frozen road and across a low stone bridge that crossed the river. Donnan mused that it would likely be the last sign of civilization for a very long time. For the moment they crossed, the bridge gave way almost immediately to dense woods, without even the shadow of a trail making its way between the trees. Where they had just left, a bustling village hugged right up against the river, a far cry from the untouched wilderness they faced. Donnan remembered an old story, something about the West always making the first to cut a new trail pay its cost with his blood. He shook his head. No time for old wive's fables, he told himself. There's work to be done.

The surveyor walked up to the forest, consulted his notebook, straightened his pack, raised his lantern, and slipped in between two trees without a second's hesitation. The rest followed suit - first the Farmer, then the Mountain Man, the Sailor, and finally Donnan bringing up the rear. Before he stepped in, he gave one last glance back.

They say most don't make it back, he thought grimly. Hogwash. It's just a day's travel in. And then he too walked out into the forest.

-----

They all walked between the thick trees of the forest. The Farmer and the Surveyor conversed in hushed voices that were barely audible over the crunch of their boots. The snow was deep, and would have come up past their shins if they weren’t walking on the frozen surface. Donnan checked his rifle again. The Sailor hurried to keep up with the flickering light of the lantern ahead.

Before an hour's time, the darkness began to wane. Soon they could begin to catch glimpses of light between the spindly bare branches. Day was beginning to break, but never a drearier day was seen. The clouds covered the sky in a flat gray covering, more uniform and featureless than the finest canvas in the East. The sun could not be seen. Not even a light patch on the featureless sky betrayed its location. The air, if it was even possible, was even colder and stiller than the night before. It caught in Donnan's throat with every breath, and formed great clouds every time he breathed it out, as though the air had been brought to life within him and was dying once again as it left.

As they continued into the morning, the trees began to thin. The forest gradually waned into a sort of long glade. Donnan saw that it made a sort of path of its own, curving up and down the steep hills. If it weren't for the Surveyor's sure path, they would have certainly missed it.

Without a word spoken between them, they continued through the morning. All that could be heard was the crunch of their boots and their heavy breathing as they labored for each step. Each time Donnan put his boot down, it seemed to collect a little bit more of the snow to carry with him until each step felt like he had hidden a lead weight in his boots.

At a time that felt like it might've been around noon, the Farmer called for a stop. They briefly paused for a dry and cold meal before carrying on. Donnan's breathing grew heavy as the hours wore on, and his steps were more labored than ever. Judging from their sluggish pace and heads that hung down low, the others weren't doing much better. Worst of all was the Sailor. He had kept up well for the first half of the morning, but he had begun to fall behind. When they had stopped, he had all but collapsed at the base of a tree, and had not fared any better afterwards. Now he was walking some fifteen paces behind the rest, and did not seem to be making up any ground. Donnan looked ahead to the Farmer, who was plodding ahead alongside the Surveyor, and had not looked back in some time.

"Farmer," Donnan called, his voice dry and hoarse. He had no energy to try and clear his throat before speaking, and the cold had sapped his mouth of moisture.

His voice, though he had not spoken softly, seemed to be captured by the stillness, swallowed before making it ten yards. The Farmer glanced back stiffly, before swiveling his shoulders and upper body to rotate his hood and look at Donnan through the opening.

Donnan wordlessly lifted one arm and pointed back at the struggling Sailor. He did not have the energy for more words. The farmer squinted at Donnan for a moment, then turned farther to follow his arm. Finally he grunted in acknowledgement and signalled for the Surveyor to stop before turning around and trudging back to the Sailor. Donnan felt a mote of triumph rise in his chest, pushing back against the chill that had taken root there. Some leader, he thought. Can’t even keep watch over his charges.

While the Farmer checked on the Sailor, Donnan used the opportunity to make his way abreast with the Surveyor. He was checking a small pocket-watch and squinting at the sky, muttering to himself under his breath. All Donnan was able to hear was "not right...something's not right..." He would unfurl a small map, worriedly page through his notebook, and then check his map again.

"Have you lost our way?" Donnan asked testily.

The Surveyor glanced at him for a moment before returning to his notebook. "No, no...the way is clear. It's just..." And he returned to muttering for a moment before speaking clearly again. "The temperature. It's too cold. This time of year, this time of day...too cold. If we don't make it by night..." He trailed off again. "Can't you feel it?"

And Donnan could. He had been, all day. The air had mellowed out somewhat during the middle of the day, but after noon it had begun to cool again. And what a chill it was - his hands felt like he had never put on gloves, and his feet and face had not had feeling since that morning. He only knew his feet were still there when he could hear the crunch of his empty boots hit the snow, and only knew his lips moved when the words left his mouth. He had never known such cold.

"And?" Donnan prodded. "Will we make it?"

The Surveyor looked at his map one more time. "Maybe. If we make haste, maybe." He glanced back at the Farmer, who had slowly walked back with the Sailor. Neither were looking good - the Farmer's beard had solidified into one solid piece, and the Sailor's face was as blue as a hanged man's. They both moved like statues brought to a semblance of life, yet had not fully left behind their cold stone flesh. Even the Mountain Man seemed to be feeling it. He had fared the best so far, seemingly used to the harsh conditions and having worn a heavy coat made of many layered furs, but even his steps were becoming labored.

All together again, they carried on.

The hours bled together in the endless rolling fields of white and sparse strands of trees. He found himself looking down to watch a stranger's legs carry him forwards, one dogged step at a time. They brought him after the Surveyor as the clearings gave way to a forest, which gave way to an endless impenetrable grove with barely a path wide enough for two to walk abreast. The trees around them leaned in eagerly, nearly covering the flat grey of the clouds with a spiderweb of dark branches.

Eventually, he realized that the dimming of the sky wasn't his eyes failing him for lack of a new scene to study, but rather that night had begun to fall.

"Ho," he called. Or at least he tried. His lips would not part, and barely a sound issued forth. Nevertheless, in the quiet and monotony of their walk, it might as well have been a shout. The Surveyor turned back to glance at him in confusion.

Unable to elaborate, Donnan lifted one arm and aimed it at the sky. The Surveyor followed it slowly with his gaze, then nodded in silent agreement. He held up a hand to signal a halt.

Donnan immediately knew that it was a mistake. The moment he stopped, the creeping deadness in his limbs immediately seized upon his joints and stilled his movements. He could only watch numbly as the surveyor dropped his pack and fumbled with the latch. The rest of the party lurched to a halt nearby. Donnan dimly noted that the Sailor was being all but carried by the Mountain Man.

It was nearly full dark now, and Donnan could barely see his breath fog in front of his face. The night seemed to fall over the land like a heavy cloth, falling between the trees and into every nook and cranny of the land. It brought the whole world pressing in around them. And with it came cold.

This new cold that made the day seem like a warm summer's afternoon. Even through his numbness Donnan could feel how the air suddenly seemed to pierce his very core, invading with every breath. It was the sort of cold that he’d only read about, that prowled the northern border, eagerly consuming anything unprepared for its clutches.

The Surveyor rifled through his pack with hands like solid paddles. Quickly he came back up with a small wooden box, perhaps the size of his hand, which he opened to reveal a bundle of small, thin sticks with one end dark. He dipped a hand into the box, pushing the sticks around with unresponsive gloved fingers that rebelled against his orders.

With difficulty, the Surveyor lifted one out of the box and gripped its end. He rotated the box and pressed the end of the stick against the long edge. In a moment, it had fallen from his fingers. Cursing under his breath, the Surveyor reached back in with a new urgency, and tried again without success. His fingers slipped from the slim stick.

The last of the light faded away, and the Surveyor was lost in the gray muddle of the darkness. Donnan just stood there, still. It felt as though he'd always been here. Just another one of the trees, ever to stand watch over the trail. He didn't feel the cold anymore. Didn't feel pain, or anger. Just tired. It would be so nice to rest, he thought. To just sit down and take a break, and try again later.

In the dark, he heard the Surveyor continue to move. There was a sound like a stick scraped against the bark of a tree, once, twice, three times. Finally, a spark blazed to life in an orange flash. The Surveyor's blue face was illuminated in the dim glow of a flame, burning a bundle of sticks clutched in his trembling hand. Finally Donnan recognized them through his thickening thoughts. Matches.

The effect was immediate. The flame of the matches produced little light and only the barest hint of heat, but it shocked his face his icy face like a roaring campfire. He sucked in a breath he didn't realize he had been holding, startled from his stupor. He had forgotten that feeling, that warmth. That warmth existed.

Around him, the light had a similar effect on the rest. He could hear the Farmer cough, and the Mountain Man shift in his steps, brought back to life by the glow. Only the Sailor didn't move.

The Surveyor had a lantern out at once, and set the tenacious flame to its wick. A new light flared to life. He donned his pack and stood slowly, lifting the lantern high. "We're close," he rasped. "One mile. Less."

Donnan rubbed his hands together. He could almost imagine he could feel them again.

They all moved as quickly as they could, barely managing half the pace they had started the day with. The going was slow, but Donnan knew that if they stopped again, the forest would have them. The path turned, once, again, then finally opened into a small clearing.

Low mounds dotted the snow-covered floor, suggesting the presence of stumps. Trees as thick as pond reeds walled the edges. In the center of the clearing stood a small cabin, sturdily built.

Donnan hurried to the door, the Farmer right behind him. He reached out a hand and caught the latch. The knob turned, but the door didn't open. Didn't budge. He leaned back, then threw his shoulder into the door. It cracked. The Farmer caught up to him, and together they hit it again. This time it gave way, slowly creaking open.

They all hustled inside. In the light of the Surveyor's single lantern, it was a humble affair. A few bunks, a table and chairs, some shelves, a low stone fireplace. It was no warmer than the outside, but nevertheless something changed when they crossed the threshold. The deadliest edge of the cold retreated, unable to pass the threshold.

Donnan crossed at once to the fireplace. There were logs in the tender, dry and old, with tinder and kindling nearby. He clumsily stacked them in the fireplace. The Surveyor brought out another match, lit it from his lantern, threw it in. He then poured oil over the logs from a small jar. The kindling caught - the logs sputtered into flame.

The cabin slowly filled with a warm orange glow. Donnan slowly sat down near the fire, almost afraid of the stillness, still remembering the strange weariness that had nearly consumed him on the trail. But the fire was warm, a real warmth that slowly began working its way through his gloves and bringing his dead limbs back to life. He was at the journey’s end. He had to remind himself that rest wouldn’t kill him.

He stayed that way for some time, just trying to stay awake. He had started shivering again - he hadn't even noticed he had stopped. He could feel the cold again, trying to seep back through his coat, but the fire pushed it to the far corners of the cabin. Every so often, he would add another log to the fire, even before the fire needed it, until it threatened to spill from the fireplace and set their shelter alight.

Eventually, he returned to himself enough to start thinking about moving. He turned to look to the rest, and was surprised to find the Farmer sitting immediately next to him, staring into the flames, his gloves off. All of them had silently gathered around the fire without him noticing. They all looked well, a ruddy color returning to their faces. Even the Sailor looked better, though the blue clung to his lips and nose.

He allowed himself to breathe a sigh of relief. They had made it.
